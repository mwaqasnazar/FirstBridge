Understanding Load Profitability Analysis

Our load profit calculator helps owner-operators make informed decisions about which loads to accept. By analyzing all costs associated with a load including fuel, tolls, maintenance, and dispatch fees, you can determine your true net profit and avoid loads that appear profitable but actually lose money.

Key Metrics Explained

Net Profit vs. Gross Revenue

Gross Revenue is the total amount you will be paid for the load. Net Profit is what you actually keep after all expenses. Many owner-operators make the mistake of only looking at gross revenue. A $3,000 load might seem attractive, but if expenses total $2,800, your net profit is only $200 — barely worth the effort.

Profit Margin Percentage

Industry experts recommend maintaining a profit margin of 15-20% for sustainable trucking operations. Consistently accepting loads below 10% profit margin puts your business at risk.

Cost Per Mile Analysis

The average owner-operator's cost per mile ranges from $1.50 to $1.80 depending on equipment type, fuel efficiency, and operating region. If your cost per mile is $1.65 and you're offered $2.00 per mile, your profit margin is approximately 17.5% — a healthy rate.

Fuel Cost Impact

Fuel typically represents 30-40% of your total operating costs. A truck averaging 6.5 MPG on a 1,200-mile load at $3.80/gallon will consume approximately 185 gallons, costing $703 in fuel alone.

When to Accept or Reject a Load

Accept These Loads:

  • Profit margin above 15% - Healthy, sustainable profit
  • Good positioning - Delivers to a strong freight market
  • Quick turnaround - Minimal wait time for pickup/delivery
  • Backhaul opportunity - Freight available for return trip

Be Cautious With:

  • Profit margin 10-15% - Acceptable occasionally, but not sustainable long-term
  • Weak market delivery - May result in expensive deadhead or low-paying backhaul
  • Tight pickup windows - Risk of detention without detention pay

Reject These Loads:

  • Profit margin below 10% - Not worth your time and equipment wear
  • Negative profit - Obviously losing money
  • No backhaul prospects - Expensive empty return significantly impacts profitability

Real-World Example

Scenario: You're offered a 1,200-mile load paying $3,000.

  • Revenue: $3,000 ($2.50/mile)
  • Fuel Cost: $703 (185 gal @ $3.80)
  • Tolls: $75
  • Maintenance Reserve: $180 (1,200 mi @ $0.15)
  • Other Expenses: $50
  • Total Expenses: $1,008
  • Net Profit: $1,992 (66.4% margin)

This is an excellent load with healthy profitability well above industry targets. Accept immediately and maintain good relationships with this broker for future loads.
